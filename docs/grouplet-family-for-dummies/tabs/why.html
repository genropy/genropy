Why grouplets

Grouplets are the GenroPy answer to forms that have to look different depending on what they are editing right now.

The problem

Real business forms are rarely uniform. A contact record needs different fields when the contact is a person, a company, or a supplier. An invoice row has one shape for goods, another for services, another for discounts. A configuration panel exposes different settings per module. The data in the record decides what the user should see, and the developer has to keep up.

The first instinct is to pile everything into one giant form and gate each section with if/else on the record type. That works for two cases and collapses by the fifth: every new variant adds branches, every change to a shared field has to be hunted down across the cascade, and the form turns into a switchboard nobody wants to touch. The second instinct is partial templates loaded by hand — a snippet here, an included page there — but those fragments do not compose: each one assumes its own datapath, its own context, its own lifecycle, and stitching them together becomes the real work.

What is missing is a unit small enough to be swapped at runtime, attached to a specific datapath, and ignorant of where it lives. That unit is the grouplet.

The shape of a grouplet

A grouplet is a modular UI fragment. You define it either as a Python @public_method handler living inside the webpage, or as a resource file under resources/grouplets/ (page-scoped) or resources/tables/<table>/grouplets/ (table-scoped). Either way, the fragment receives a pane and fills it with widgets. The whole engine is in resources/common/gnrcomponents/grouplet/grouplet.py — gr_loadGrouplet picks the source, mixes it in, and hands you the box.

Every grouplet binds to a datapath through the value= parameter, and inside the fragment widgets reference fields with relative paths like ^.street or ^.email. That single rule is what makes grouplets compose: two grouplets on the same page get two different subtrees of the Bag, so their ^.name never collide. Swap the resource and the new fragment lights up against the same subtree, picking up whatever the previous one left behind.

The family

grouplet

The base widget. Loads one fragment at a datapath, by handler or by resource. Reach for it whenever a single chunk of UI has to vary with the data underneath.

groupletChunk

An inline summary rendered through a template plus an edit button that pops a memoryDataEditor. Use it inside grids and dashboards where a compact preview opens a focused editor.

groupletForm

A grouplet wrapped in a form with its own status, save lifecycle, and validation. Use it when the fragment edits a record and you want save/load semantics rather than raw Bag mutation.

groupletPanel

A two-region layout — tree or multibutton on one side, the selected grouplet on the other. Use it for settings screens, admin consoles, or any place where the user picks a section and edits it.

groupletWizard

A linear sequence of grouplets walked step by step, with optional summary page. Use it for onboarding flows, multi-step configurations, or any process the user has to complete in order.

groupletGrid

A repeating grouplet, one row per record, with reactive cells bound to the Bag. The newest member of the family — reach for it when you would have used a tablehandler grid but the row needs to be a real form layout, not just columns.

Topic Grid

A topic folder (a subdirectory of grouplets sharing a theme) rendered as a grid of expandable cells, one per child grouplet. Use it when a configuration screen has a fixed set of independent sections that should all be visible at once.

Reactive flow

The loop is the whole point. Once the grouplet is mounted, the Bag at the datapath is the source of truth: every widget inside the fragment subscribes to its own relative path, every edit writes back to that subtree, and only the widgets whose path actually changed re-render. Swap the resource and the same Bag is handed to a different fragment without losing state.
